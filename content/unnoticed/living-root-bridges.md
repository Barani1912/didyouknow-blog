In the dense, rain-soaked valleys of Meghalaya, India, engineering is not done with concrete or steel, but with patience and living biology. The local Khasi and Jaintia tribes have perfected the art of growing "living root bridges" (Jingkieng Jri) from the aerial roots of the *Ficus elastica* (Indian rubber tree).

When a bridge is needed, the tribes place hollowed-out betel nut tree trunks across a river to guide the flexible rubber tree roots. Over 10 to 15 years, the roots grow across the stream, eventually taking root on the opposite bank and forming a sturdy walkway.

Over time, rather than decaying like wooden bridges, these living structures grow stronger. As the roots mature and intertwine with stones and dirt, they can easily support the weight of over 50 people at once and last for several centuries. They are a masterclass in regenerative, sustainable design.
